The 2024–25 period has introduced sweeping reforms to property investment legislation across Australia. These multifaceted changes impact taxation mechanics, tenant rights, and purchasing eligibility, reshaping the financial calculus for investors nationwide.

<Section icon="scale" title="Negative Gearing Changes">

While widespread abolition of negative gearing did not eventuate, the regulatory framework governing allowable deductions has tightened considerably. The core mechanism remains—allowing investors to offset property losses against ordinary income—but the calculation basis for structural components and high-wear assets has been modified.

Notably, travel expenses related to inspecting and maintaining residential investment properties remain strictly non-deductible, reinforcing the 2017 legislation. Furthermore, depreciation deductions for previously used plant and equipment (like dishwashers or blinds installed by a prior owner) are completely quarantined for residential properties, meaning secondary buyers must rely entirely on capital works deductions (Division 43) for structural depreciation.

</Section>

<Section icon="shield" title="Capital Gains Tax Modifications">

Building upon the announcements preceding the 2026 transitional period, early legislative indicators emphasize a shift toward cost-base indexation models for high-net-worth investors. The 50% CGT discount remains intact for individuals holding assets longer than 12 months, but reporting scrutiny has intensified.

The ATO has implemented advanced data-matching programs specifically targeting property sales. Investors failing to accurately proportion main residence exemptions—especially in scenarios where a property was partially rented out, utilized for a home business, or placed on short-term rental platforms (like Airbnb)—face immediate audit triggers.

</Section>

<Section icon="building2" title="Rental Law Updates">

State-by-state harmonization of residential tenancies legislation has been a major theme. Key updates heavily favor tenant security and property minimum standards:

- **Rent Bidding Bans:** Strictly enforced prohibition on soliciting or accepting rent offers above the advertised price.

- **Frequency of Increases:** Rent increases are now universally capped to once every 12 months across almost all jurisdictions.

- **Minimum Housing Standards:** Implemented mandatory requirements for heating, ventilation, and energy efficiency. Properties failing these standards cannot legally be leased.

- **No-Grounds Evictions:** Phased removal of 'no-cause' lease terminations, requiring landlords to provide specific, statutory reasons (e.g., selling the property, major renovations) to end a tenancy.

</Section>

<Section icon="globe" title="Foreign Buyer Restrictions">

To alleviate domestic housing pressure, federal surcharges on foreign purchases have tripled. The Foreign Investment Review Board (FIRB) application fees for residential property acquisitions have increased drastically, paired with a doubling of the vacancy fee for foreign-owned dwellings left unoccupied for more than six months of the year.

These policies are designed to heavily disincentivize land banking by non-residents and funnel foreign capital strictly toward new housing developments rather than established dwellings.

</Section>

<InfoCard title="Impact on Different Investor Types">

### Owner-Occupiers

Generally insulated from taxation changes, but benefit indirectly from reduced foreign competition in established housing markets. Must be careful if utilizing part of the home for income-generating activities to protect full CGT exemptions.

### Retail Investors (Individuals)

Faced with higher compliance costs regarding minimum property standards. Reduced yield flexibility due to strict 12-month caps on rent reviews. Cash flow modeling is now vastly more important than relying purely on capital growth.

### SMSF Trustees

Navigating severe penalties for non-arm's length expenditure (NALE). While commercial property within SMSFs remains largely unchanged, residential holdings require rigorous documentation and independent valuations.

### Corporate Investors & Trusts

Subject to tightening land tax thresholds across various states, particularly regarding absentee owner surcharges and the aggregation of land values across multiple entities.

</InfoCard>
